## Summary Sheet

The Governor's office has asked how allowing self-driving, cooperating cars on I-5, I-90, I-405 and SR-520 would affect traffic performance in Thurston, Pierce, King, and Snohomish counties, and specifically whether there is a tipping point, whether equilibria exist, and under what conditions a lane should be dedicated to autonomous vehicles (AVs).

We build a three-layer model. **Layer 1 (mechanism)** represents the interaction between human-driven (H) and self-driving/cooperating (S) vehicles through a **2×2 time-headway matrix**: a human follower's headway is bounded below by human reaction time regardless of the leader (h₀), an AV following a human is modestly faster (αh₀), and an AV following another cooperating AV is much faster due to V2V coordination and platooning (βh₀, β≪1). Assuming vehicle types are randomly interleaved with AV penetration rate p, the expected headway is a quadratic in p, and single-lane capacity is C(p) = 3600/h̄(p) veh/hr. **Layer 2 (demand-side tipping point)** combines C(p) with the officially given fact that 8% of daily volume occurs in the peak hour, and applies the classic Bureau of Public Roads (BPR) delay function to the degree of saturation X = D/(n·C(p)). **Layer 3 (dedicated-lane decision)** models the choice AVs face between a dedicated AV-only lane and the general mixed lanes as a **Wardrop user-equilibrium** traffic-assignment problem, which we prove has a unique equilibrium (delay on the dedicated lane is monotonically decreasing, and delay on the general lanes monotonically increasing, in the fraction of AVs routed to the dedicated lane).

Because the official Excel spreadsheet was not available, all applied numbers in this paper (lane counts, ADT, headway ratios α and β) are explicitly labeled **illustrative/example values**, chosen only to be order-of-magnitude plausible so that the model's *logic* can be verified computationally; the mathematical structure, however, is fully general and can be re-parameterized with real WSDOT data.

**Key computed findings** (illustrative parameters; see Solution and Sensitivity sections for exact values):
- Capacity gain from AV penetration is **convex and accelerating**: roughly +25% at p=0.5 but +117% at p=0.9 relative to p=0, under our example headway parameters — most of the benefit arrives late, not linearly.
- **Equilibria exist and are unique** in both the classical two-branch (free-flow/congested) sense implicit in the flow-density relationship, and in the explicit Wardrop lane-choice sense we prove and verify numerically.
- A **tipping point is not an intrinsic property of p alone** — it only appears when a corridor's peak demand already exceeds the human-only capacity; we show numerically that a light/moderate-demand corridor never exhibits a sharp tipping point as p increases, while a heavy/oversaturated corridor exhibits one at a specific p* solving D = n·C(p*).
- The **dedicated-lane decision** has a clean threshold structure: below a critical p_thresh, dedicating a lane wastes capacity (confirmed by a p=0 sanity check where dedicating is always worse); above it, dedicating strictly reduces total system delay. Remarkably, we derive **and numerically confirm** that, in the corner-solution regime, p_thresh is *scale-invariant with respect to the absolute demand level D* — it depends only on lane count n, the number of dedicated lanes k, and the headway parameters, not on how heavy traffic is. Sensitivity analysis shows this threshold is most sensitive to the number of lanes (0.53 at n=2 down to 0.16 at n=6) and comparatively robust to the exact platooning-headway assumption.

Policy recommendation: prioritize AV-lane pilots on wider corridors (more lanes → lower penetration needed to justify dedication), avoid premature lane dedication before roughly a quarter of traffic is autonomous (under our example parameters), and prioritize telemetry collection to calibrate the headway matrix, since the model's qualitative structure is robust but its precise numeric predictions are not.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Problem Restatement

The Governor of Washington State has requested an analysis of allowing self-driving, cooperating cars on I-5, I-90, I-405, and SR-520 in four counties. The analysis must address, as the percentage of self-driving cars rises from 10% to 50% to 90%:

1. How traffic-flow effects change with AV penetration, lane count, and peak/average volume;
2. Whether equilibria exist;
3. Whether there is a tipping point where performance changes markedly;
4. Under what conditions lanes should be dedicated to AVs; and
5. Any other policy recommendations suggested by the analysis.

The model must explicitly represent (a) cooperation among self-driving cars and (b) interaction between self-driving and human-driven vehicles, and must be applied to the roads of interest using the data provided in the problem's Excel spreadsheet.

### 1.3 Modeling Strategy Overview

We considered three modeling strategies (discussed further in the accompanying research log) and selected a **headway-based capacity model** combined with a **BPR-type demand/delay layer** and a **Wardrop equilibrium lane-choice layer**, because this combination (i) gives cooperation among AVs and AV–human interaction a direct, mechanistic representation (via an asymmetric headway matrix) rather than an ad hoc functional form, (ii) yields closed-form or numerically solvable answers to all five questions the Governor asked, and (iii) uses standard, well-established, checkable tools from transportation engineering (Highway Capacity Manual style headway-capacity relationships, the 1964 BPR delay function, Wardrop's principle) rather than a generic "black-box" machine-learning or multi-criteria-decision method that would have no mechanistic connection to car-following physics.

## 3. Model Development

### 3.1 Notation

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| p | AV ("self-driving, cooperating") penetration rate, p ∈ [0,1] |
| h₀ | baseline human-follower time headway (s) |
| α | headway ratio for an AV following a human leader, h_HS = αh₀ |
| β | headway ratio for an AV following an AV leader (platooning), h_SS = βh₀ |
| h̄(p) | expected time headway of a randomly chosen vehicle pair, given penetration p |
| C(p) | single-lane capacity at penetration p (veh/hr/lane) |
| n | number of lanes (one direction) |
| k | number of lanes dedicated to AVs (0 ≤ k < n) |
| D | peak-hour directional demand (veh/hr) |
| X | degree of saturation, X = D/(capacity) |
| a, b | BPR delay-function parameters |
| q | fraction of AV demand routed to the general (non-dedicated) lanes, under lane dedication |

### 3.2 Layer 1 — Headway-Based Capacity of Mixed Traffic

Define the leader→follower headway matrix (leader type first):

h_HH = h₀,  h_SH = h₀ (follower is human ⇒ human reaction time governs regardless of leader),
h_HS = αh₀ (leader human, follower AV),  h_SS = βh₀ (leader AV, follower AV).

Under assumption A2 (i.i.d. Bernoulli(p) interleaving), the probability that a random adjacent pair has leader type i and follower type j is the product of the marginal probabilities. Hence the expected headway is

  h̄(p) = (1−p)² h_HH + (1−p)p · h_HS + p(1−p) · h_SH + p² h_SS
      = (1−p)² h₀ + (1−p)p · αh₀ + p(1−p) · h₀ + p² βh₀.               (1)

Single-lane capacity follows from the standard headway–capacity relationship (capacity = number of vehicles that can cross a point per unit time at the minimum sustainable headway):

  C(p) = 3600 / h̄(p)   [veh/hr/lane].                    (2)

For n identical shared lanes, total directional capacity is n·C(p) (A2 implies each lane sees the same statistical mixture).

**Special cases and sanity checks.** C(0) = 3600/h₀ recovers the pure human-traffic base saturation flow rate; C(1) = 3600/(βh₀) is the theoretical ceiling under full, perfectly cooperative automation. Equation (1) is quadratic in p, so C(p) is a smooth, strictly increasing rational function on [0,1] whenever β<α<1 — we verify its monotonicity and convexity numerically in Section 5.

### 3.3 Layer 2 — Demand, Peak-Hour Loading, and the BPR Delay Function

Given an average daily traffic volume ADT for one direction, and the problem-supplied fact that 8% of daily volume occurs at peak, peak-hour directional demand is

  D = 0.08 · ADT_dir.                            (3)

(Equation (3) uses the 8% figure exactly as given in the official problem statement; ADT_dir itself is, absent the official spreadsheet, an illustrative input in this paper.)

For n lanes with composition p, define the degree of saturation

  X(p) = D / (n · C(p)),                          (4)

and the BPR travel-time index

  TT(X) = 1 + a·Xᵇ,   a=0.15, b=4.                     (5)

Equation (5) is convex and only mildly super-linear for X<1 but rises steeply once X approaches or exceeds 1 — this is the mechanism by which a genuine "tipping point" can appear (Section 3.4).

### 3.4 What Counts as a "Tipping Point" in This Model

Because C(p) itself is smooth (Section 3.2), a sharp, threshold-like change in system performance cannot come from the capacity function alone. It can, however, come from the **interaction** of a fixed peak demand D with a smoothly rising capacity n·C(p): if D exceeds the human-only capacity n·C(0), the corridor starts in an oversaturated regime (X(0)>1, TT(0)≫1) and there exists a unique p* solving

  D = n · C(p*)                              (6)

(existence and uniqueness follow because n·C(p) is continuous and strictly increasing on [0,1]) at which the corridor transitions from oversaturated (X>1, delay exploding under the 4th-power BPR term) to undersaturated (X<1, delay near free-flow). We treat p* as the *tipping point* for that corridor: below it, small further gains in p produce only marginal delay relief because the system remains queued; at and above it, the same increment in p produces a qualitative jump from a "gridlocked" to a "free-flowing" regime. If D ≤ n·C(0), no such p* exists in (0,1) and the corridor never experiences a tipping point — it simply gets incrementally less congested as p rises. This is a *conditional*, not universal, existence statement, and Section 5 verifies both cases numerically.

### 3.5 Layer 3 — Dedicated-Lane Decision as a Wardrop Equilibrium

Suppose k of the n lanes are reserved for AV-only use, leaving n−k general lanes. Total directional demand D splits into human demand D_H=(1−p)D and AV demand D_S=pD. Because only AVs can choose between the dedicated lane and the general lanes, let q ∈ [0,1] be the fraction of D_S that uses the general lanes (so 1−q uses the dedicated lane).

**Dedicated-lane delay:**
  X_ded(q) = (1−q)D_S / (k·C_SS),  C_SS = 3600/(βh₀),
  TT_ded(q) = 1 + a·X_ded(q)ᵇ.                       (7)

**General-lane delay** (composition of the general lanes is now p_gen = qD_S / (D_H+qD_S)):
  X_gen(q) = (D_H+qD_S) / ((n−k)·C(p_gen)),
  TT_gen(q) = 1 + a·X_gen(q)ᵇ.                       (8)

**Wardrop equilibrium** q* satisfies TT_ded(q*) = TT_gen(q*) if an interior solution exists, or is a corner solution q*=0 (dedicated lane still weakly preferred even when it carries *all* AV demand) or q*=1 (dedicated lane weakly worse even when it carries *none*).

**Proposition (existence & uniqueness).** TT_ded(q) is non-increasing in q (more AVs diverted away from the dedicated lane can only lighten it) and TT_gen(q) is non-decreasing in q (more AVs added to the general lanes can only load it further); hence g(q)=TT_ded(q)−TT_gen(q) is non-increasing, so it has at most one interior zero, and the equilibrium (interior or corner) exists and is unique by the intermediate value theorem. This is verified numerically in Section 5.4.

**Dedication decision rule.** Total system delay (a proxy for total vehicle-hours of delay) is
  Total_A(p) = D·TT(X(p))     [no dedication, Eq. 4–5]        (9)
  Total_B(p) = (1−q*)D_S·TT_ded(q*) + (D_H+q*D_S)·TT_gen(q*)  [dedication]  (10)

Dedicating is beneficial exactly when Total_B(p) < Total_A(p). Because both are continuous and Total_A(0)<Total_B(0) (an AV-only lane is pure waste when p=0) while Total_A(1)=Total_B(1) (dedication is irrelevant once traffic is homogeneous), a threshold p_thresh generally exists.

**Scale-invariance proposition.** In the corner-solution regime q*=0, expand (9)–(10): the constant ("1×") terms of the BPR function are D·1 on one side and D_S·1+D_H·1=D on the other, which cancel exactly (by conservation D=D_H+D_S). The remaining terms are all proportional to D^{b+1} (via X^b · D). Hence, **as long as the system stays in the q*=0 corner regime, the sign of Total_B − Total_A — and therefore p_thresh — does not depend on the absolute demand level D**, only on n, k, and the headway parameters. This closed-form property is verified numerically to 10 significant figures across a demand range spanning 10× in Section 5.5, and constitutes, to our knowledge, a genuinely derived (not assumed) structural result of the model.

### 5.2 Do Equilibria Exist?

Two distinct, complementary senses of "equilibrium" are addressed:

1. **Classical flow–density equilibrium.** As in any fundamental-diagram-based traffic model, a given sub-capacity flow generally corresponds to two possible steady states — a low-density free-flow branch and a high-density congested branch — while flows above capacity have no steady-state solution (only a growing queue). Our BPR/degree-of-saturation formulation (Section 3.3) is the standard reduced-form way of representing this: X<1 corresponds to the free-flow branch (small TT), X→∞ corresponds to the queued regime (TT→∞). This confirms equilibria exist below capacity and cease to exist (in a steady-state sense) once demand exceeds capacity, motivating why capacity increases from AV adoption matter.
2. **Wardrop lane-choice equilibrium** (Section 3.5) — proved to exist and be unique whenever a dedicated lane is introduced, and confirmed numerically: at p=0.5 (Table 0 parameters, Moderate demand, n=4, k=1), TT_ded(q) is monotonically **decreasing** from 1.0293 (q=0) to 1.0000 (q=0.8–1.0), and TT_gen(q) is monotonically **increasing** from 1.0241 (q=0) to 1.1582 (q=1.0) — the two curves cross exactly once, confirming the proposition of Section 3.5.

**Conclusion: yes, equilibria exist in both senses, and in the lane-choice sense the equilibrium is provably unique.**

*(Figure 3b — `fig3_dedicate_boundary.png`, right panel — dedication threshold vs. lane count.)*

## 7. Strengths and Weaknesses

### Strengths
- Every parameter in the model has a direct physical or economic interpretation (a time headway, a demand/capacity ratio, a route-choice equilibrium condition) — there is no unexplained "black-box" function.
- Cooperation among AVs and AV–human interaction are represented by *distinct, separately identifiable* mechanisms (β vs. α) rather than folded into a single generic "AV effect" parameter, directly matching what the problem statement asks the model to address.
- The five questions posed by the Governor's office (penetration effects, existence of equilibria, tipping points, lane dedication, and policy implications) are each answered by a specific, traceable piece of the same unified framework rather than by five disconnected ad hoc analyses.
- Two non-trivial analytical results (convexity of C(p); scale-invariance of the dedication threshold in the corner regime) were *derived*, not assumed, and independently confirmed numerically — including a dedicated verification script re-running the threshold calculation at ADT_dir values differing by 10×.
- The Wardrop equilibrium's existence and uniqueness are proven analytically (via monotonicity) and then checked numerically against the proof, rather than merely asserted.
- All delay/capacity building blocks (HCM-order headway, 1964 BPR default parameters) are standard, checkable transportation-engineering tools, not fitted or invented functional forms.

### Weaknesses
- **No real calibration data.** The official WSDOT Excel spreadsheet was unavailable; α, β, and the illustrative ADT/lane-count scenarios are examples chosen to be plausible, not measurements. Sensitivity analysis shows the quantitative capacity-gain magnitude is the most exposed conclusion to this gap.
- **One-dimensional, single-segment abstraction.** The model does not represent ramps, weaving sections, intersections (e.g., the I-5/I-90 interchange explicitly mentioned in the problem), or network-level route choice; it analyzes one homogeneous directional segment at a time.
- **i.i.d. random mixing assumption (A2).** Real AVs might coordinate to platoon together more than random interleaving would predict (which would make our capacity gains a conservative lower bound), or conversely traffic-management policy might force more interleaving than assumed.
- **Static/steady-state, not dynamic.** The BPR delay function is a widely used but reduced-form static approximation; it does not resolve stop-and-go shockwave dynamics, queue-dissipation timing, or transient behavior during the peak period, which a full car-following simulation (e.g., a two-class cellular automaton) would capture but at a much higher parameter and computational burden.
- **Only one dedicated-lane configuration analyzed in depth (k=1 of n).** The model generalizes to arbitrary k, but the applied analysis emphasizes k=1; a full k-sweep for each n would be a natural extension.

---

## 8. Conclusion

Using a headway-based capacity model, a BPR-type demand/delay layer, and a Wardrop-equilibrium lane-choice model, we find that (1) capacity gains from increasing AV penetration are convex and back-loaded — modest at 10%, large at 90% — under our example headway parameters; (2) equilibria exist in both the classical flow-density sense and, provably uniquely, in the dedicated-lane choice sense; (3) a genuine tipping point in system performance exists only for corridors that are already at or beyond human-only capacity, and does not appear on already-uncongested corridors; and (4) dedicating a lane to AVs is only beneficial once AV penetration exceeds a threshold that is (in the regime studied) insensitive to the absolute level of traffic demand but strongly sensitive to the number of available lanes — favoring wider corridors as earlier pilot-dedication candidates. Because official data for the Washington State corridors named in the problem were not available to this analysis, every applied number here should be treated as a demonstration of the modeling *method*, to be re-run once real WSDOT lane-count, ADT, and (ideally) AV headway telemetry become available; the mathematical structure of the model does not depend on which numbers are plugged in.

**Additional policy recommendations suggested by the analysis:**
- Prioritize telemetry collection to calibrate β (platooning headway) before committing to large capital investments justified by specific capacity-gain percentages, since this is the parameter the model is most sensitive to.
- Sequence AV-lane pilots by lane count, starting with the widest corridors (e.g., multi-lane I-5 segments) rather than narrower segments (e.g., portions of SR-520), since our sensitivity analysis shows the dedication threshold is substantially lower where more lanes are available.
- Avoid dedicating a lane before AV penetration is well-established (illustratively, above roughly one-quarter of traffic under our example parameters) to avoid the "empty dedicated lane" waste our p=0 corner-case check quantified.
- Because a tipping point in delay only appears on corridors already near or above capacity, monitoring should flag those specific corridors for priority AV-adoption incentives, rather than applying a uniform statewide policy.
