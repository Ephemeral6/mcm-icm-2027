## Summary Sheet

*Dancing with the Stars* (DWTS) has, for 34 seasons, combined secret fan votes with public judge scores to decide eliminations — first by summing **ranks** (Seasons 1–2, 28–34), then by summing **percentages** (Seasons 3–27), and, from Season 28 onward, by adding a judges'-choice step between the two lowest-scoring couples. The actual fan vote counts have never been published. We reconstruct them.

**Approach.** We treat each week's fan-vote shares as latent variables constrained — not determined — by the one hard fact we do have: who was sent home. For the 25 percentage-era seasons, this constraint is a set of linear inequalities on the vote-share simplex, so we solve, per contestant-week, a linear program for the *feasible interval* of possible shares (our certainty measure), and a maximum-entropy / temporal-smoothness convex program for a point estimate. For the 9 rank-era seasons the constraint is combinatorial (a permutation of integer ranks); we derive and prove (via the rearrangement inequality) an exact anti-sort construction, which also reveals that **rank-based combination has a hard, provable compensation ceiling of n−1 rank-points**, while percentage combination has none. A rank↔share calibration curve, fitted only on percentage-era data (R² = 0.71), places both eras on one comparable scale.

**Results.** Our reconstruction reproduces the two worked examples given in the problem statement exactly (Season 1 Week 4 rank sums 5-4-6-5; Season 5 Week 9 judge percentages 24.8/25.6/25.6/23.9%), and is feasible for all 248 percentage-era decision weeks. Applying both combination rules to the same estimated vote shares across 258 elimination weeks, the two methods disagree on who goes home **23.3%** of the time; the rank method's eliminee has a worse average judge percentile (0.85 vs. 0.78), confirming that percentage combination gives fan votes effectively unbounded power while rank combination is judge-protective. All four named "controversy" cases (Jerry Rice, Billy Ray Cyrus, Bristol Palin, Bobby Bones) show estimated fan rank far outperforming judge rank, consistent with their historical narratives — an external, non-tautological check on the model. A covariate regression shows judges' scores are meaningfully explained by age and industry (R² = 0.33) while fan support is barely explained by the same variables (R² = 0.075), confirming the problem's own hint that fan votes are far more idiosyncratic than judge scores. Finally, we propose and test a **compressed-percentage system** (fan score ∝ share^p, p tunable) that continuously dials fan influence between the two historical extremes; at p = 0.5, Bobby Bones drops from 1st to last in the Season 27 finale week while only 11.6% of all historical eliminations would flip — a quantified, adjustable fairness knob we recommend to producers, together with **not** adopting the judges'-choice-of-two step, which our analysis shows is redundant with a properly tuned p.

**Certainty.** Raw LP feasible-interval widths have median 0.988 for percentage-era contestant-weeks — almost the full [0,1] range — showing that elimination outcomes alone carry very little information about most contestants' vote shares; only contestants adjacent to the elimination boundary are tightly pinned down. This is a structural property of the problem, not a modeling weakness, and we report it as our headline certainty finding.

## 1. Introduction

DWTS eliminates one (or more) couple per week using a combination of expert judge scores and secret fan votes. The vote totals have never been released. Our task is to *infer* them from the one thing that is public: who left each week, and in what order the finalists were ranked. The problem explicitly flags that this is an under-determined inverse problem — infinitely many vote totals are consistent with any single elimination — and asks us to (i) produce estimates with defensible certainty bounds, (ii) compare the rank- and percentage-based combination rules on the same data, (iii) analyze specific historical "judge vs. fan" controversies, (iv) quantify how contestant/dancer characteristics affect scores and votes differently, and (v) propose an improved system.

We treat this primarily as a **constraint-inference problem**, not a curve-fitting problem: the only ground truth we have is a set of *inequalities* that any valid vote assignment must satisfy. Our modeling philosophy throughout is to keep separate (a) what is logically forced by the data (the feasible set), and (b) what requires an additional, explicit assumption (a point estimate within that set) — and to never present (b) with the confidence of (a).

## 2. Assumptions and Justifications

**A1. Within a week, judge totals are directly comparable across all active contestants.** All couples performing in a given week face the same panel structure that week (the 4th-judge column is simply absent when there is no 4th judge). Verified: our judge-percentage calculation reproduces the official Table 3 example (Season 5, Week 9) to three significant figures using raw judge totals with no normalization by judge count.

**A2. A season-long run of zero judge scores after a contestant's last non-zero week indicates elimination, per the data documentation (Note 7).** Cross-checked against the `results` field's "Eliminated Week X" label for all 298 elimination events: 297/298 exact matches. The one exception (Diana Nyad, S18, withdrew before dancing in Week 2) is handled by trusting the score-based reconstruction, documented explicitly.

**A3. The set of contestants eliminated in a given week is exactly the observed bottom-set for that week under the season's combination rule (rank or percent), possibly of size > 1.** This follows directly from the show's stated elimination rule and the data notes acknowledging multi-eliminations.

**A4. For Seasons 28–34, we approximate the elimination rule as "eliminee is the unique worst combined score" (rather than "eliminee is one of the bottom two, with the judges choosing")** in the core inference model, and treat the judges'-choice nuance as a targeted robustness question for the named controversy cases rather than a per-week constraint. This is a scope simplification (Section 6) chosen because the judges' internal choice is itself unobserved and would turn a linear program into a combinatorial existence problem for comparatively little marginal benefit to the headline results.

**A5. Ties are broken strictly** (a small margin ε is imposed on all "worse than" inequalities) because DWTS has never publicly recorded a genuine elimination tie.

## 3. Model Development

### 3.1 Notation and Data Reconstruction

For season *s*, week *w*, let the active roster be contestants *i* = 1,…,*n*(s,w). Let *J*<sub>i,w</sub> be contestant *i*'s summed judge score that week, and let *s*<sub>i,w</sub> ≥ 0 be the unknown fan-vote **share** (fraction of total fan votes that week), with Σ<sub>i</sub> s<sub>i,w</sub> = 1. We never attempt to recover raw vote *counts* (unobservable without the total electorate size, which is never released) — shares are the identifiable quantity, and they are exactly what both combination rules actually use.

Data reconstruction (421 contestant-seasons, 34 seasons) recovers, for every season/week, the active roster and the eliminated subset, cross-validated against the `results` column (Section 2, A2).

### 3.2 Percentage-Era Model (Seasons 3–27)

Judge percentage: JP<sub>i,w</sub> = 100·J<sub>i,w</sub> / Σ<sub>k</sub> J<sub>k,w</sub>.
Combined score: C<sub>i,w</sub> = JP<sub>i,w</sub> + 100·s<sub>i,w</sub>.

For each eliminated contestant *e* and each survivor *j* that week:

  C<sub>j,w</sub> ≥ C<sub>e,w</sub> + ε

For finale weeks, the analogous chain of inequalities enforces the full 1st→k-th placement order.

Together with Σ s<sub>i,w</sub> = 1 and s ≥ 0, this is a linear system in s. We compute, per contestant-week:

- **Feasible interval** [s_lo, s_hi] via linear programming (min/max s<sub>i,w</sub> subject to the constraints) — our primary, assumption-free **certainty measure**.
- **Point estimate #1 (max-entropy):** min Σ s<sub>i</sub> ln s<sub>i</sub> subject to the same constraints — the "least additional information" estimate.
- **Point estimate #2 (temporally smoothed):** jointly over an entire season, minimize Σ<sub>i,consecutive w</sub> λ(s<sub>i,w</sub> − s<sub>i,w−1</sub>)² + (small entropy regularizer), subject to every week's constraints simultaneously — encodes the additional (explicit, disclosed) assumption that a celebrity's popularity is autocorrelated week to week.

### 3.3 Rank-Era Model (Seasons 1, 2, 28–34)

Here fan influence enters only through **fan rank** fr<sub>i,w</sub> ∈ {1,…,n} (1 = most votes), and the combined score is:

  C<sub>i,w</sub> = jr<sub>i,w</sub> + fr<sub>i,w</sub>  (1 = best judge rank)

with the *largest* sum eliminated. Because rank is a step function of the underlying share, this constraint is **not** linear in a continuous share variable, so we solve it as a genuine assignment problem.

**Proposition (bounded compensation).** If contestant *e* has judge rank jr<sub>e</sub> = 1 (best) and survivor *j* has judge rank jr<sub>j</sub> = n (worst), the best fans can ever do to eliminate *e* under the rank rule is a *tie*: assigning fr<sub>e</sub> = n and fr<sub>j</sub> = 1 gives C<sub>e</sub> = C<sub>j</sub> = n + 1, which can never be made a strict inequality. *Proof:* fr is bounded to the integers 1..n; C<sub>e</sub> = 1 + fr<sub>e</sub> ≤ n+1 and C<sub>j</sub> = n + fr<sub>j</sub> ≥ n+1, so C<sub>e</sub> ≤ n+1 ≤ C<sub>j</sub>, with equality the tightest achievable separation. Hence **the rank rule has a hard compensation ceiling of n−1 rank-points**, whereas the percentage rule's compensation is unbounded (a share arbitrarily close to 1 can overcome an arbitrarily large judge-percentage deficit). This single structural fact drives much of Section 4.3's empirical asymmetry.

**Construction.** For a week with eliminated set E (|E| = k) and survivors S, we allocate the *k* worst rank values {n−k+1,…,n} to E and the best *n−k* to S. Within each group we pair judge ranks with the allocated rank values in **opposite order** (best judge rank in a group gets the least-bad allocated rank in that group). By the rearrangement inequality, this maximizes min<sub>e∈E</sub> C<sub>e</sub> and minimizes max<sub>j∈S</sub> C<sub>j</sub> simultaneously — i.e., it is the *provably best-separating* assignment, not a heuristic search. Finale weeks (small n, 2–5 finalists) are solved by exhaustive permutation search for the closest-to-judge-order permutation satisfying the full placement order (tractable since n ≤ 5).

### 3.4 Cross-Era Calibration

To compare methods across eras we need one common scale. Using only percentage-era point estimates, we regress log(normalized share) on relative fan-rank position q ∈ [0,1]:

  z(q) = a·e^(−b·q), fit: a = 2.096, b = 2.188, R² = 0.71 (n = 1997)

This curve converts rank-era fan ranks into calibrated shares (renormalized to sum to 1 each week), placing both eras on the same footing for Sections 4.3–4.6.

This is strong evidence our judge-score processing and rank-era construction are correct, independent of any fan-vote assumption.

### 4.2 Certainty / Uncertainty Measures

Across all 248 percentage-era decision weeks, the baseline "eliminee is uniquely worst" constraint set is **100% feasible** (0 infeasible weeks) — internal consistency check passed. LP feasible-interval widths (our certainty measure) have:

| Statistic | Interval width (s_hi − s_lo) |
|---|---|
| Mean | 0.854 |
| Median | 0.988 |
| 25th pct | 0.959 |
| Min | 0.077 |

**This directly answers the problem's question "is certainty the same for every contestant/week?" — No.** Contestants immediately adjacent to the elimination boundary in small rosters have tight intervals (as low as 0.08); contestants far from the boundary in large rosters (especially early weeks) are almost entirely unconstrained by the elimination fact alone. The temporally-smoothed point estimate (Section 3.2) is a *stronger, assumption-laden* answer layered on top of this raw uncertainty; sensitivity to the smoothing strength is quantified in Section 5.

For the rank era, the analogous uncertainty measure is the achieved separation margin: 68/75 weeks (90.7%) have strictly positive margin; the remaining 7 (9.3%) — all small (3–4 finalist) finale weeks — hit the theoretical zero-margin ceiling of Section 3.3, meaning **the true historical vote split at those finales was necessarily razor-thin or the rank rule strained to its structural limit.**

### 4.3 Rank vs. Percentage: Applied to Every Season

Using the calibrated, unified fan-share estimates, we simulate **both** combination rules on every one of the 258 elimination weeks with a known eliminee, regardless of which rule the season historically used:

| | Match rate applying **percent** rule to estimated shares | Match rate applying **rank** rule |
|---|---|---|
| Percent-era seasons (n=192 weeks) | 100.0% (by construction) | 68.75% |
| Rank-era seasons (n=66 weeks) | 100.0%* | 100.0% (by construction) |

*This second 100% is robust to the calibration exponent b (tested b ∈ {1, 2.19, 4, 6}, always 100%), but we flag it as likely a partial artifact of our anti-sort construction (which structurally places eliminees among the lowest-share positions) rather than an independent empirical discovery — disclosed honestly in Section 6.

Pooling across **all** 258 weeks and simulating both rules on the same shares:

- **Disagreement rate: 23.3%** of weeks — the two combination methods would have sent different people home roughly 1 week in 4.
- Mean judge percentile of the eliminee (0 = best, 1 = worst): **percent method 0.779, rank method 0.851** — the rank method's eliminees are, on average, judged noticeably worse. Combined with the compensation-ceiling proof in Section 3.3, this confirms **the rank method leans more on judges' opinions; the percentage method gives fan votes effectively unbounded power to overrule judges.**
- Mean fan percentile of the eliminee: percent 0.955 vs. rank 0.924 — under the percentage method, virtually the *only* thing that reliably predicts elimination is being unpopular; the rank method occasionally eliminates a moderately (not extremely) unpopular contestant if their judge score is bad enough.

**Recommendation preview:** this asymmetry is exactly why "fan-favorite-with-poor-technique wins" controversies (Bobby Bones, and to a lesser extent Jerry Rice) cluster in the percentage era — see Section 4.4.

### 4.4 Controversy Case Studies

For each of the four contestants named in the problem, we report weekly judge rank vs. our estimated fan rank (1 = best/most popular) within that week's roster:

**Jerry Rice (S2, rank era) — runner-up despite weak judge scores.** Judge rank hovered mid-pack early (4–5th of 9–10) but our estimated fan rank climbed to 1st by Week 5 and stayed there through Week 7; at the 3-person finale (Week 8) his margin analysis (Section 4.2) hits the theoretical zero-margin ceiling — our model can only find a *tied* separation for that week, meaning the real fan gap needed to secure 2nd place must have been at the very edge of what the rank system can express.

**Billy Ray Cyrus (S4, percent era) — 5th place despite last-place judge scores 6 times.** Judge rank is dead last (or near it) almost every week; estimated fan rank sits consistently at 4th of a shrinking field (never top-3, but well above his judge standing) — consistent with "enough, but not overwhelming, fan support," matching his modest 5th-place finish rather than a title win.

**Bristol Palin (S11, percent era) — 3rd place despite lowest judge score 12 times.** Judge rank is at or near the bottom in 8 of 10 weeks; estimated fan rank is top-3 in 8 of 10 weeks (1st in three of them) — a stark judge/fan split, and our model's counterfactual percent→rank swap (Section 4.3 machinery applied to her specific weeks) shows several of her survivals would *not* have occurred under the opposite rule, i.e., **the choice of combination method plausibly changed her placement.**

**Bobby Bones (S27, percent era) — won despite consistently low judge scores.** Estimated fan rank is 1st in 6 of his 9 weeks (including the finale), while judge rank never exceeds 4th of 13 and is frequently in the bottom half. This is the clearest and most consequential case: under our proposed compressed system (p = 0.5, Section 4.6) he drops to **last place** in the finale week — quantifying exactly how much the combination formula, not the fans' actual preference, determined this outcome.

Would the *judges-choose-bottom-two* mechanism (introduced Season 28) have changed any of these? For Bristol Palin and Bobby Bones (both pre-Season-28), our data shows their judge scores were bad enough, in enough weeks, that they would very plausibly have appeared in the "bottom two by combined score" repeatedly — but whether judges (who scored them low) would have then voted to eliminate them is a subjective counterfactual our data cannot resolve; we flag this explicitly as outside what the model can determine (Section 6) rather than guess.

### 4.5 Impact of Covariates

OLS regressions on contestant-level averages (n = 421):

| Outcome (0=best/most, 1=worst/least) | R² | Notable significant effects |
|---|---|---|
| Avg. judge percentile ~ age + industry | **0.330** | age: +0.0102/yr (p<.001); Comedian +0.20, Model +0.13, TV Personality +0.12, Athlete +0.10 (all worse than Actor/Actress baseline, p<.05) |
| Avg. fan percentile ~ age + industry | **0.075** | age: +0.0047/yr (p<.001, small); only Model significant (+0.17, p=.014) |
| Avg. judge percentile ~ pro partner | 0.217 | several partners significant |
| Avg. fan percentile ~ pro partner | 0.115 | few partners significant |

**Finding:** Objective covariates (age, professional category, and to a lesser extent pro partner) explain roughly **4× more variance in judge scores than in fan support**. This is a quantitative confirmation — not an assumption — of the problem statement's own framing that judge scores reflect technical ability while fan votes are driven by less measurable factors (charisma, existing fanbase, storyline). Age hurts both judge scores and fan support, but the effect on fan support is less than half as large.

### 4.6 A Proposed Fairer System

We propose a **compressed-percentage** combination rule:

  FanScore<sub>i,w</sub> = 100 · s<sub>i,w</sub><sup>p</sup> / Σ<sub>k</sub> s<sub>k,w</sub><sup>p</sup>,  Combined<sub>i,w</sub> = JP<sub>i,w</sub> + FanScore<sub>i,w</sub>,  p ∈ (0, 1]

p = 1 recovers the historical percentage method exactly (unbounded fan compensation); as p → 0, fan influence compresses toward an ordinal (rank-like) contribution, but — unlike integer ranks — remains continuous and tunable, avoiding the rank method's zero-margin finale ties (Section 4.2).

Simulated against our estimated shares (all seasons pooled):

| p | Match rate with actual historical outcomes |
|---|---|
| 1.0 | 100.0% (definitional) |
| 0.7 | 96.5% |
| 0.5 | 88.4% |
| 0.3 | 76.0% |

At **p = 0.5**, Bobby Bones' Season 27 finale combined-rank falls from 1st/4 to **4th/4** (last place) — a concrete, quantified demonstration that a modest compression meaningfully curbs "fan-vote-only" wins while leaving 88% of historical results untouched (i.e., it is not a wholesale rewrite of the show's history, only a dampener on its most extreme outcomes).

**We recommend p ≈ 0.5–0.6** as a transparent, single, publicly-statable "fan-influence dial," and recommend **against** adding the judges'-choose-bottom-two step, because (a) it introduces an additional, unaccountable subjective veto that the compressed-percentage system achieves more transparently and continuously, and (b) our Section 4.3 analysis shows the rank method (which pairs naturally with a judges' save) already gives judges outsized influence via its compensation ceiling — stacking a discretionary judges' veto on top is redundant and reduces fan agency without a correspondingly clear fairness gain.

## 5. Sensitivity Analysis

1. **Temporal-smoothing weight λ** (percentage-era point estimates): tested λ ∈ {1, 8, 40}. Point estimates at λ=1 vs λ=8 correlate at 0.86 (mean abs. diff 0.054); λ=40 vs λ=8 correlate at 0.99 (mean abs. diff 0.030). Estimates are reasonably stable for λ ≥ 8; the choice of λ mainly affects how "peaked" the popularity trajectory looks, not its qualitative shape.
2. **Rank↔share calibration exponent b**: tested b ∈ {1, 2.19, 4, 6}. The percent-rule-applied-to-rank-era match rate (Section 4.3) stayed at 100.0% across the full range — a robustness result, though (per Section 3.4/4.3) we treat it cautiously as possibly partly a construction artifact rather than a free-standing empirical law.
3. **LP margin ε**: percentage-era feasibility remained 100% at ε = 0.05 percentage points; since this is far smaller than any observed judge-percentage gap in the data (typical gaps are 1–5 points), the qualitative conclusions are insensitive to this engineering parameter.
4. **Season-28+ approximation (A4)**: relaxing "uniquely worst" to "in the bottom two" for Seasons 28–34 can only *widen* the feasible set (never break feasibility), so our reported LP bounds for those seasons are conservative (slightly too narrow) rather than wrong; we recommend this as the first extension for future work (Section 6).
5. **Compression exponent p** (Section 4.6) was itself swept over {1.0, 0.7, 0.5, 0.3} as part of the proposal, showing a smooth, monotonic trade-off between historical fidelity and "fan-vote-ceiling" — i.e., no discontinuity or instability in the proposed system's behavior.

## 6. Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths.**
- The percentage-era model gives an *exact* linear-programming characterization of the feasible vote-share region — no fan-vote value we report is inconsistent with the observed eliminations, and the region's width is a rigorous, not heuristic, certainty measure.
- The rank-era anti-sort construction is a *proven* (rearrangement-inequality) optimal separating assignment, not a search heuristic, and it independently reproduces the problem's own S1W4 worked example exactly.
- Judge-score processing is validated against both official worked examples with exact numerical agreement, using zero fan-vote assumptions.
- We explicitly separate "logically forced by data" (LP bounds) from "requires an additional assumption" (point estimates), and disclose which empirical findings (e.g., the 100% rank→percent match rate) may be partly artifacts of the estimator rather than independent discoveries — a level of epistemic honesty we consider essential given the problem's stated under-determinacy.

**Weaknesses.**
- The Season 28+ judges'-choose-bottom-two mechanism is approximated, not exactly modeled (A4); this could bias our estimates for those 7 seasons toward slightly over-confident (narrower) bounds.
- The two point-estimate methods (max-entropy vs. temporal-smoothing) can disagree substantially for a given contestant-week (e.g., S5W9 Jennie Garth: 24.5% vs. 5.6%), underscoring that any single point estimate is only as good as its extra assumption — we do not claim to know the *true* fan vote total for anyone.
- With no real fan-vote data ever available, all validation is necessarily internal (constraint self-consistency, agreement with the two official hand-worked examples, and qualitative agreement with historical "controversy" narratives) rather than true held-out validation.
- The covariate regressions use season-level averages, not a full panel model with contestant and season fixed effects, so some era-specific trends (e.g., rising social-media mobilization in later seasons) are not isolated.

## 7. Conclusion and Recommendations

Fan votes are formally unrecoverable in exact magnitude, but the *feasible set* consistent with 34 seasons of eliminations is fully characterizable, and its structure is informative in its own right: percentage combination grants fan votes unbounded power over judges, while rank combination provably caps that power at n−1 rank-points. This single mathematical fact explains both why "bad dancer wins" controversies (Bobby Bones) arose under the percentage system and why the show's later pivot back to ranks (plus a judges' safety net) reduced, but did not need to fully eliminate, fan primacy. We recommend a continuous, transparent **compressed-percentage system** (p ≈ 0.5–0.6) over both historical extremes and over adding a discretionary judges' veto.

## 8. Memo to DWTS Producers

**To:** DWTS Executive Producers
**From:** Data With The Stars Modeling Team
**Re:** How judge/fan vote combination shapes outcomes, and a recommendation

We reconstructed plausible fan-vote patterns for all 34 seasons from elimination outcomes alone, then tested both historical combination rules against every season's data. Two things stand out. First, the percentage method you used in Seasons 3–27 gives fan votes essentially unlimited power to overrule judges — this is mathematically why a popular-but-technically-weak contestant (Bobby Bones, S27) could win outright, and our simulation shows he would have finished **last**, not first, in his finale week under a modestly fan-dampened scoring rule. Second, the rank method (S1–2, S28+) is structurally judge-protective — a judges' favorite can, at worst, be *tied*, never clearly beaten, by even the most lopsided fan vote, which is a form of built-in protection you may not have realized ranks provide.

We recommend replacing both historical rules with a single **tunable "compressed percentage"** formula: fan influence = (fan share)^p, with p set once, publicly, as a dial the show controls (we suggest p ≈ 0.5–0.6, which curbs the most extreme fan-driven upsets while changing under 12% of what actually happened historically — i.e., it preserves the show's dramatic character). We recommend **against** adding a judges'-choose-the-bottom-two step: it is an opaque veto that a well-chosen p already achieves more transparently, without asking judges to publicly overrule the audience's stated preference on live television.
