# The Goodgrant Challenge: A Value-Added, Diminishing-Returns Framework for Optimal Multi-Year Educational Philanthropy

## Summary Sheet

The Goodgrant Foundation seeks to allocate $100 million per year for five years across a pool of candidate U.S. colleges and universities in order to maximize the positive effect of its money on student educational performance, while defining an investment ranking, a per-school dollar amount, an appropriate return-on-investment (ROI) concept, and a funding duration for each school.

We formulate this as a **three-layer decision problem**. **Layer 1 — screening.** Because raw outcome levels (graduation rate, post-graduation earnings) are heavily confounded by a school's student-body characteristics (admission selectivity, share of low-income/Pell students), ranking schools on raw outcomes would systematically reward already-elite institutions that do not need philanthropic capital and would not use it at the margin. We instead regress observed outcomes on intake characteristics and use the **residual ("value-added")** as a school-specific measure of how effectively the institution converts its actual student population into strong outcomes — i.e., its "demonstrated potential for effective use of private funding." A composite **Investment Potential Score (IPS)** blends value-added on completion and on earnings with a low-income access/mission term and a loan-default risk penalty.

**Layer 2 — allocation.** We model each school's expected annual gain in completions as a function of the dollars it receives, y_i(x) = a_i(1 − e^{−x/k_i}), which is increasing and strictly concave (diminishing marginal returns), with a per-school cap that bounds any single grant at a fraction of the school's own operating budget (an absorptive-capacity / diversification constraint). Maximizing the sum of these concave, separable functions subject to a linear budget constraint is a classical problem whose KKT conditions collapse to a **water-filling algorithm**: every funded school is pushed to the point where its marginal return equals a common shadow price λ (the budget's marginal value). We solve this by bisection on λ and **independently verify** the result against a generic nonlinear optimizer (`scipy.optimize`, SLSQP), confirming agreement to within $0.30 on a $100,000,000 budget.

**Layer 3 — duration.** Rather than fixing each school's funding horizon a priori, duration is allowed to **emerge endogenously**: each year the Foundation observes a noisy new outcomes signal, performs an empirical-Bayes update of its belief about each school's effectiveness, and re-solves the Layer-2 allocation problem. A school continues to receive funding as long as its updated marginal return clears that year's shadow price; it exits the active candidate pool after two consecutive zero-funding years (a persistence rule that filters transient statistical noise).

Because the official dataset was unavailable, we validate the model on a **synthetic candidate pool of 60 schools** built to mimic plausible ranges of real IPEDS/Scorecard variables; all numeric results below are explicitly **illustrative**, not computations on real institutions. On this synthetic pool, a representative Year-1 solution funds **28 of 60** candidate schools, fully exhausts the $100M budget, and is confirmed optimal against an independent solver. A five-year simulation shows funding duration positively correlated with the initial IPS score (Spearman-style correlation ≈0.60), confirming the mechanism concentrates capital on schools with demonstrated, persistent effectiveness. Sensitivity analysis over scoring weights, absorptive-capacity assumptions, per-school caps, and total budget shows the model behaves monotonically and consistently with economic intuition (e.g., completions-per-dollar strictly decreases as total budget rises, confirming diminishing returns are operating as designed).

We conclude that a **charitable ROI** should not be defined as "value produced exceeds dollars spent" (a financial break-even framing), because an optimal allocation under diminishing returns will, by construction, push spending past that point for many recipients. Instead we recommend the Foundation track **marginal ROI relative to the pool's shadow price** — i.e., "is this grant still buying more impact per dollar than any alternative use of the same dollar" — as the operationally correct concept of return for a mission-driven funder.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Problem Restatement

The Goodgrant Foundation will donate $100,000,000 per year for five years (2016–2021) to U.S. undergraduate institutions, with the goal of producing "the highest likelihood of producing a strong positive effect on student performance." The Foundation asks for: (1) a prioritized 1-to-N list of candidate schools; (2) an investment amount per school; (3) a charity-appropriate definition of ROI; (4) a funding duration per school; and (5) a two-page letter to the CFO explaining the strategy and the ROI concept.

### 1.2 Restatement as a Modeling Problem

We decompose the problem into three linked sub-questions:

1. **Who** — how do we identify schools with genuine potential to convert additional private funding into better student outcomes, as distinct from schools that already produce strong outcomes purely because of the students they admit?
2. **How much** — given a fixed annual budget, how should dollars be allocated across the selected schools to maximize aggregate impact, given that any single institution's capacity to productively absorb funding is finite?
3. **How long** — should a school's funding be fixed for the full five years, or should it be re-evaluated, and on what basis?

### 1.3 Data Availability Note

The official data package `ProblemCDATA.zip` (IPEDS UID candidate list, College Scorecard cohort data, data dictionary) referenced in the problem statement was **not available** in the environment used to produce this paper. All quantitative results in this paper are therefore computed on a **synthetic candidate pool** of 60 schools constructed in Python to have plausible marginal distributions (Pell share, selectivity, six-year completion rate, ten-year median earnings, institutional budget) loosely anchored to publicly known U.S. higher-education ranges. **No result in this paper should be read as describing a real institution.** The purpose of this exercise is to demonstrate that the proposed mathematical framework is internally consistent, numerically verifiable, and produces sensible, explainable behavior — the framework itself, not any specific numeric output, is the deliverable.

## 2. Assumptions and Justifications

**A1. The primary outcome proxy for "educational performance" is incremental completions (additional students who finish their degree) attributable to the grant.**
*Justification:* The problem statement leaves "performance" undefined, so a proxy must be chosen. Completions are the most direct, least-lagged, least macroeconomically confounded outcome available in IPEDS/Scorecard-type data (unlike post-graduation earnings, which are observed only ~10 years later and are heavily influenced by regional labor markets). Earnings are retained as a **secondary/cross-check** signal in the scoring layer, not the primary optimization target.

**A2. Each school's production of incremental completions is an increasing, strictly concave function of the dollars it receives.**
*Justification:* Institutional capacity — faculty hiring, advising staff, facilities, administrative bandwidth — is finite and expands slowly; the easiest, cheapest interventions (emergency micro-grants, tutoring) are typically deployed first, and each additional dollar buys a smaller improvement thereafter. We specifically choose the exponential-saturation form y(x)=a(1−e^{−x/k}) rather than a power form a·x^θ (θ<1) because the latter has infinite marginal return as x→0, which would make the optimum degenerate (funnel the "first dollar" disproportionately); the exponential form has a **finite, well-defined marginal return at x=0**, which is economically more defensible.

**A3. "Demonstrated potential for effective use of private funding" is measured as value-added — the residual of outcomes after controlling for a school's intake characteristics (selectivity, low-income share) — not as the raw outcome level.**
*Justification:* Raw outcome levels are confounded by selection: more selective, lower-Pell schools mechanically post higher completion rates regardless of institutional effectiveness. Funding on raw levels would reward schools that do not need philanthropic capital and would not show the marginal effect the Foundation is trying to buy. The residual isolates the component of performance attributable to the institution itself.

**A4. Any single grant is capped at a fraction of the recipient's own annual operating budget.**
*Justification:* This serves two purposes: (i) it is a crude but defensible proxy for absorptive capacity — an institution cannot productively deploy a grant that dwarfs its own budget in a single year; (ii) it enforces portfolio diversification, limiting the Foundation's downside exposure to any single institution's execution risk, consistent with standard grant-making governance practice.

**A5. Funding duration is not fixed ex ante; a school remains funded as long as its (Bayesian-updated) marginal return clears the pool's market-clearing shadow price, and exits the active pool only after two consecutive zero-funding years.**
*Justification:* A single noisy annual cohort observation is not a reliable basis for a permanent decision (cohort sizes, especially at smaller schools, and year-to-year economic conditions introduce substantial measurement noise). A two-year persistence rule is a standard noise-filtering device that avoids prematurely severing a relationship with a genuinely effective school due to one bad data year, while still allowing capital to be reallocated away from a persistently underperforming recipient.

**A6. Population-level cross-sectional value-added scores serve as an informative but not definitive prior on each school's true effectiveness; the Foundation updates this belief with its own observed outcomes over time.**
*Justification:* Cross-sectional data reflect population averages and are not immune to noise or omitted confounders; treating the initial score as a fixed truth would ignore the more direct signal the Foundation itself accumulates once it begins funding (and observing) a specific school.

**A7. All quantitative results in this paper use a synthetic candidate pool, explicitly labeled, and are not to be interpreted as real institutional data or conclusions.**
*Justification:* Data-integrity requirement given the unavailability of the official dataset; stated here as an assumption governing how every subsequent number should be read.

## 3. Model Development

### 3.1 Notation

For each candidate school *i* = 1, …, N:

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| $E_i$ | undergraduate enrollment (cohort size) |
| $P_i$ | share of students receiving Pell grants (low-income access proxy) |
| $\sigma_i$ | selectivity index ($1-$admit rate) |
| $C_i$ | observed six-year completion rate |
| $R_i$ | median earnings 10 years after entry |
| $D_i$ | student loan default rate |
| $\text{Budg}_i$ | institutional annual operating budget |
| $x_i$ | dollars allocated to school $i$ in a given year |
| $y_i(x_i)$ | expected incremental completions produced by $x_i$ |

### 3.2 Layer 1 — Value-Added Regression and Investment Potential Score

We regress each raw outcome on intake characteristics across the candidate pool:

$$C_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \sigma_i + \beta_2 P_i + \varepsilon_i, \qquad VA^{C}_i \equiv \varepsilon_i = C_i - \hat C_i$$

$$R_i = \gamma_0 + \gamma_1 \sigma_i + \gamma_2 P_i + \eta_i, \qquad VA^{R}_i \equiv \eta_i = R_i - \hat R_i$$

By construction of ordinary least squares, $VA^{C}_i$ and $VA^{R}_i$ are (numerically) orthogonal to $\sigma_i$ and $P_i$ — the value-added residual is purged of the selection confound. The **Investment Potential Score** is a weighted composite of standardized ($z$-scored) components:

$$IPS_i = w_1\, z(VA^{C}_i) + w_2\, z(VA^{R}_i) + w_3\, z(P_i) - w_4\, z(D_i)$$

with baseline weights $w = (0.35, 0.25, 0.25, 0.15)$ reflecting: primary weight on demonstrated completion effectiveness, secondary weight on earnings effectiveness, an explicit mission-alignment term (serving low-income students), and a risk penalty (loan default rate, a proxy for student financial distress and program quality risk). Weights are a policy choice of the Foundation, not a mathematical necessity; §5.1 tests robustness to alternative weightings.

### 3.3 Layer 2 — Concave Production Function and Optimal Static Allocation

We posit

$$y_i(x_i) = a_i\left(1 - e^{-x_i/k_i}\right), \qquad a_i = \gamma \cdot \big(IPS_i - \min_j IPS_j + 0.3\big) \cdot \frac{E_i}{1000}, \qquad k_i = \delta \cdot \text{Budg}_i$$

where $a_i$ (the ceiling of achievable annual completions gain) scales with both demonstrated potential and cohort size (a school can only produce as many additional completions as it has students to graduate), and $k_i$ (the saturation scale) scales with the school's own budget (absorptive capacity). $\gamma,\delta$ are calibration constants (§4.1 states the illustrative values used).

Since $y_i'(x)=(a_i/k_i)e^{-x/k_i}>0$ and $y_i''(x)<0$, each $y_i$ is strictly increasing and concave. The **single-year allocation problem** is

$$\max_{\{x_i\}} \sum_i y_i(x_i) \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \sum_i x_i \le B, \quad 0 \le x_i \le x_i^{\max}=\text{cap}\cdot \text{Budg}_i$$

This is a separable concave maximization with one linear coupling constraint — the classical structure solved by **water-filling**. The Lagrangian KKT stationarity condition requires a single shadow price $\lambda \ge 0$ such that for every school

$$
x_i(\lambda)=
\begin{cases}
0 & \text{if } y_i'(0)=a_i/k_i \le \lambda\\
\min\!\big(x_i^{\max},\; k_i \ln\frac{a_i/k_i}{\lambda}\big) & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
$$

and $\lambda$ is chosen so that $\sum_i x_i(\lambda)=B$. Because $\sum_i x_i(\lambda)$ is monotonically non-increasing in $\lambda$, this scalar equation is solved reliably by bisection (we use Brent's method). **Every funded school ends up at the same marginal return $\lambda$** (or at its absorptive cap) — a school receives a *larger* grant only because it can absorb more before its marginal return falls to $\lambda$, not because it is "ranked higher" per se. This is the precise mathematical sense in which the model answers "how much."

### 3.4 Layer 2b — A Charity-Appropriate ROI Definition

A financial ROI (value produced / dollars spent, net of 1) is not the right lens for a mission-driven funder, because an *optimal* allocation under diminishing returns deliberately spends past the point where average value equals cost (see §4.3 for a concrete demonstration). We therefore define two complementary metrics:

- **Marginal Social ROI** (the operationally correct concept for allocation decisions):
$$mROI_i(x_i) = y_i'(x_i) \quad \text{[incremental completions per marginal dollar]}$$
A grant is worth making, at the margin, exactly when $mROI_i(x_i) \ge \lambda$ (i.e., not worse than the best alternative use of the same dollar within the candidate pool). This is what the water-filling solution equalizes across all funded schools.
- **Average Social ROI** (a reporting/communication metric):
$$ROI_i = \frac{y_i(x_i)}{x_i} \quad \text{[completions per dollar invested, over the whole grant]}$$
which can optionally be monetized by multiplying by an assumed present value of the lifetime earnings/social premium per additional completion, $V$ (an explicit **policy parameter**, not a market price — we use $V=\$400{,}000$ purely as an illustrative example in §4.3, not an authoritative estimate).

### 3.5 Layer 3 — Multi-Year Adaptive Reallocation (Endogenous Duration)

Let $\hat a_i^{(t)}$ denote the Foundation's belief about school $i$'s effectiveness scale at the start of year $t$. Each year:

1. **Observe** a noisy signal $s_i^{(t)} = a_i^{true} + \epsilon_i^{(t)}$, $\epsilon_i^{(t)}\sim \mathcal N(0,\sigma_{obs,i}^2)$ (the year's Scorecard cohort update).
2. **Update** via precision-weighted (empirical-Bayes) combination:
$$\text{Var}^{(t)} = \Big(\tfrac{1}{\text{Var}^{(t-1)}}+\tfrac{1}{\sigma_{obs,i}^2}\Big)^{-1}, \qquad \hat a_i^{(t)} = \text{Var}^{(t)}\Big(\tfrac{\hat a_i^{(t-1)}}{\text{Var}^{(t-1)}} + \tfrac{s_i^{(t)}}{\sigma_{obs,i}^2}\Big)$$
so uncertainty about each school shrinks monotonically as more of the Foundation's own funding history accumulates.
3. **Re-solve** the Layer-2 water-filling allocation with updated $\hat a_i^{(t)}$ over the currently active candidate pool.
4. **Duration/exit rule:** a school is dropped from the active pool after **two consecutive years** of receiving $x_i=0$ (persistence filter against single-year noise).

A school's realized funding duration is therefore the number of years its updated marginal-return estimate clears the year's shadow price $\lambda^{(t)}$ — an emergent property of the model, not a separately chosen parameter.

The regression of completion rate on selectivity and Pell share shows the expected confound in the raw data (correlation of raw completion rate with selectivity ≈0.47), while the value-added residual is, by OLS construction, orthogonal to selectivity (correlation ≈0.00) — confirming the residualization step removes the intended confound before scoring proceeds.

### 4.4 Why "financial ROI" is the wrong lens: a diagnostic check

Using the illustrative monetization $V=\$400{,}000$ per additional completion (an explicit, clearly-labeled example parameter, not an authoritative estimate of the lifetime value of a bachelor's degree), the dollar-weighted average of $(V\cdot y_i(x_i)/x_i - 1)$ across the 28 funded schools is **≈ −23.7%** — i.e., the *average* dollar of Year-1 funding, valued this way, does not "break even" financially. This is not a modeling error; it is the expected, correct behavior of an optimum under diminishing returns: the water-filling solution deliberately spends *past* the point where a grant "pays for itself" in monetized terms, continuing until every funded school's *marginal* return equals the common shadow price $\lambda$. This is exactly why we recommend the Foundation adopt **marginal ROI relative to $\lambda$**, not "does this grant pay for itself," as its operational ROI concept (§3.4).

## 5. Sensitivity Analysis

**5.1 Investment Potential Score weights.** We recomputed the funded list under four alternative weightings representing different Foundation priorities (outcomes-only; mission-heavy; earnings-heavy; risk-heavy). Spearman-style rank correlation with the baseline ranking ranged from 0.858 to 0.952 (all >0.85), and the funded-school set overlapped the baseline funded set by 24–25 of 28 schools in every alternative. The ranking is **directionally robust** to reasonable weighting choices but not perfectly invariant — the residual sensitivity reflects a genuine value judgment (how much to weight mission/access vs. pure outcome effectiveness) that the model surfaces for the Foundation's board to decide, rather than resolving algorithmically.

**5.2 Per-school investment cap.** Tightening the cap from 20% to 5% of a school's own budget forces more diversification (44 vs. 28 schools funded) but reduces total completions (156.4 vs. 190.6) — a direct, monotone efficiency-vs-diversification trade-off. Above roughly 35% the cap ceases to bind for any school in this synthetic pool and results converge (27 schools, 192.8 completions), confirming the cap operates only where intended.

**5.3 Absorptive-capacity scale ($\delta$).** Larger $\delta$ (slower per-dollar saturation) monotonically reduces both the number of schools funded (36 at $\delta=0.15$ down to 21 at $\delta=0.75$) and total completions (365.6 down to 99.8) — consistent with the intended mechanics of the concave production function.

**5.4 Total annual budget.** Completions-per-\$1M **strictly decreases** as the budget rises (2.515 at \$25M → 1.505 at \$250M), the clearest possible confirmation that the diminishing-returns assumption (A2) is actually governing model behavior rather than being a cosmetic label.

Taken together, the sensitivity analysis shows the model is **numerically stable and monotone** with respect to every calibration choice tested, and that the one dimension with genuinely material sensitivity (scoring weights) reflects an irreducible policy choice rather than a modeling defect.

## 6. Strengths and Weaknesses

### Strengths
- **Addresses selection bias directly.** By scoring on value-added residuals rather than raw outcomes, the model avoids the common failure mode of funding already-elite, already-well-resourced institutions.
- **Provably optimal allocation, not a heuristic.** The water-filling solution is independently verified against a generic nonlinear solver to seven-figure precision, giving confidence the "how much" answer is a genuine optimum of the stated mathematical problem, not an ad hoc rule.
- **Duration emerges endogenously** from the same optimization logic used for allocation, rather than being a separately guessed parameter — directly answering the "how long" requirement with a mechanism, not an assumption.
- **Reframes ROI correctly for a charity.** The model explicitly demonstrates (§4.4) why financial break-even framing is the wrong concept for an optimal philanthropic allocation, and proposes an operational alternative (marginal ROI vs. shadow price).
- **All numerical claims are independently checked** (regression orthogonality check, optimizer cross-validation, budget-conservation checks, monotonicity checks across four sensitivity dimensions) rather than asserted.

### Weaknesses
- **No real data.** Every numeric result is produced on a synthetic candidate pool; the *mechanism* is validated, but no specific school, dollar amount, or ranking in this paper reflects a real institution and none should be used for a real funding decision.
- **Production function form is an assumption, not an empirical fit.** The exponential-saturation form is chosen for its qualitative properties (finite marginal return at zero, diminishing returns, boundedness); its true shape for real institutional funding is an empirical question this paper cannot answer without real longitudinal grant-outcome data.
- **Calibration constants ($\gamma, \delta$, cap %, noise variances) are heuristically chosen**, not estimated from data, and materially affect quantitative (though not qualitative/directional) results.
- **The model does not address institutional-level data churn** (mergers, closures, renamings) that complicates a real multi-year IPEDS/Scorecard panel.
- **The two-page CFO letter requirement creates real tension with model complexity**; communicating a three-layer optimization framework in two pages to a non-technical reader requires simplification that was only partially executed here (see the letter below) and would need further iteration in an actual submission.

## 7. Conclusion

We propose a three-layer framework — (1) a value-added Investment Potential Score that isolates genuine institutional effectiveness from student-body selection effects, (2) a concave-production-function water-filling algorithm that optimally allocates a fixed annual budget across selected schools with provable (solver-verified) optimality, and (3) a Bayesian multi-year re-evaluation mechanism from which each school's funding duration emerges endogenously — as a mathematically defensible answer to the Goodgrant Foundation's request. We recommend the Foundation adopt **marginal social ROI relative to the pool's shadow price** as its operational definition of return, since financial break-even framing is demonstrably the wrong lens for an optimal diminishing-returns allocation. Because the official dataset was unavailable, all specific numeric results presented here are illustrative validations of the framework's internal mathematical consistency on synthetic data; applying the framework to the real IPEDS/College Scorecard candidate list, with parameters recalibrated on real institutional budgets and longitudinal outcome histories, is the necessary next step before any real allocation decision could be made.

## Letter to the CFO

*(Reproduced here as required by the problem statement; length target ≤ 2 pages.)*

Dear Mr. Chiang,

Our team recommends that the Goodgrant Foundation adopt a three-step investment strategy for the five-year, \$100 million/year education initiative.

**Step 1 — Find schools that make good use of money, not just schools that already look good.** Many colleges have high graduation rates simply because they admit students who were already likely to graduate — funding those schools would not change much. We instead score every candidate school on how much *better* it performs than similar schools serving a similar student population (controlling statistically for selectivity and the share of low-income students). This "value-added" score is what we use to identify genuine potential for your money to make a difference, alongside your Foundation's access mission (share of low-income students served) and a risk check (student loan default rates).

**Step 2 — Give each selected school as much money as it can productively use, and no more.** Every dollar produces a smaller improvement than the last (a school can only hire so many advisors, build so many labs, in one year), and no single grant should be so large that it distorts a school's normal operations or concentrates too much risk in one place. We model this with a standard diminishing-returns curve and solve mathematically for the dollar amount to each school that maximizes total additional college completions across your whole portfolio, subject to a cap of no more than 20% of a school's own annual budget per grant. We verified this solution against an independent optimization method and confirmed it is mathematically optimal to within pennies on a \$100 million budget.

**Step 3 — Let funding duration follow the evidence, not a fixed calendar.** Rather than committing every school to a flat five-year grant, we recommend re-evaluating each recipient annually as new outcome data arrives, using a statistically disciplined updating rule that avoids overreacting to one noisy year. A school continues receiving funding as long as its updated performance keeps it competitive with the rest of your candidate pool; it is only removed after two consecutive underperforming years. In our illustrative simulation, roughly a third of candidate schools earned funding for all five years by consistently demonstrating results, while roughly half never cleared the bar to begin with, and a smaller group had funding adjusted mid-course — this is the adaptive behavior we recommend building into the real program.

**On "ROI" for a charity.** We do not recommend measuring success as "did this grant pay for itself in dollar terms," because a well-designed allocation will intentionally keep funding a school past the point of dollar break-even, as long as that school's next dollar still does more good than any alternative use of it in your portfolio. We instead recommend tracking **marginal return** — the additional impact produced by the next dollar to a given school — against the "shadow price" of your overall budget, which is a rigorous, portfolio-consistent way to know when a school has reached the point where money is better spent elsewhere.

**Important caveat.** Because the official IPEDS/College Scorecard dataset was not available to us when preparing this analysis, every specific dollar figure and school ranking in the accompanying report is an illustrative example built on synthetic data, intended solely to demonstrate that the mathematical method works and behaves sensibly. Before any real dollars are committed, the same method should be re-run on your actual candidate school data, and the model's calibration constants should be re-estimated from real institutional budgets and multi-year outcome histories.
