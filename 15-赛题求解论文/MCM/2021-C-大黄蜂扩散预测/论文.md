# Confirming the Buzz about Hornets: A Framework for Spread Prediction and Report Triage

## Summary Sheet

The 2019 discovery of an Asian giant hornet (*Vespa mandarinia*) nest in British Columbia, followed by thousands of public sighting reports in Washington State, created two linked problems for state regulators: (1) how far and how fast might the species spread, and (2) how should a state agency with limited investigative capacity prioritize thousands of mostly-mistaken public reports.

We build two complementary models joined by a shared operational layer.

**Spread model.** We treat the invasion front as a stochastic reaction–diffusion process. The queen's known 30 km annual dispersal range (given in the problem) pins down a random-walk diffusion coefficient (D = L²/4T = 225 km²/yr); combined with a logistic local growth assumption this yields the classical Fisher–KPP asymptotic wave speed c\* = 2√(rD). We augment this mechanistic "continuous" spread with a stratified-diffusion Monte Carlo layer representing rare, long-distance, human-mediated jumps (e.g., via cargo/vehicles), which is the standard mechanism used in the invasion-ecology literature for exactly this kind of "two envelope" spread pattern. In 2000 illustrative simulation replicates, the **median** predicted colonized radius after 10 years tracks the deterministic diffusion prediction closely (300 km under illustrative mid-range parameters), while the **upper tail** (95th percentile) is 55% wider, driven almost entirely by rare jump events. This directly answers the "can spread be predicted, with what precision" question: **typical-case prediction is fairly precise; worst-case prediction is inherently much less precise**, and the gap is driven by parameters (jump rate, jump distance) that cannot be pinned down without real detection data.

**Report-triage model.** We frame "likelihood of a mistaken classification" as a probabilistic classification problem, combining structured fields (date, distance to nearest known detection) with text-derived features from the Notes field (a keyword/diagnostic-mention score standing in for a TF-IDF or lexicon-based feature set) in a regularized, class-weighted logistic regression with probability calibration. We chose logistic regression over more complex classifiers (random forest, deep NLP) because the real dataset is expected to be severely class-imbalanced with very few confirmed positives, because government resource-allocation decisions require an interpretable model, and because the model must support cheap incremental updates. On an illustrative synthetic dataset built to mimic the qualitative structure described in the problem (N=4440 reports, 3% assumed positive prevalence, deliberately overlapping class distributions), the fitted model achieves ROC-AUC = 0.982 and, at a threshold tuned for 90% recall, precision = 0.522 — i.e., investigating the model's flagged reports would be roughly 10–20× more efficient at finding true positives than investigating reports at random (lift ranges from 3.3× to 17.5× depending on the investigative budget). We use these calibrated probabilities directly for triage (greedy ranking, equivalent to the optimal fractional-knapsack solution under near-equal investigation costs), for a principled model-update cadence (weekly in the active season, monthly in the dormant season, derived from an expected-backlog bound), and — combined with a closed-form confidence-of-absence calculation and a Solow (1993) sighting-gap hypothesis test — for a quantitative, jointly-verified criterion for declaring eradication.

All numeric results above use illustrative parameter values (clearly flagged throughout) in the absence of the official dataset; the methodology, however, is fully specified and directly applicable once real data are obtained.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Background

In September 2019, a nest of *Vespa mandarinia* — the world's largest hornet species and an effective predator of European honeybee colonies — was discovered and destroyed on Vancouver Island, Canada. Subsequent public alarm generated thousands of sighting reports to the Washington State Department of Agriculture (WSDA), the overwhelming majority of which are misidentifications of native insects. The state must decide, with limited investigative capacity, which reports merit costly field follow-up, while simultaneously trying to understand and predict how far the species might spread if an established population exists.

### 1.2 Restatement of the Problem

We must address:

1. Whether — and with what precision — the spatial/temporal spread of the pest can be predicted.
2. A model that predicts the probability that a given public report is a **misidentification** (equivalently, the probability it is a true positive), using only the fields available in the official dataset (and optionally images).
3. How the classification model should be used to prioritize investigative resources.
4. How the model should be updated as new reports arrive, and how often.
5. What statistical evidence would justify declaring the pest eradicated from Washington State.

### 1.3 Our Approach at a Glance

We separate the problem into two model families that use fundamentally different data and mechanisms, then join them through a shared decision layer:

```
 Biological dispersal facts       Public report records (structured + text [+ image])
  (30 km/generation, univoltine)              |
        |                                     v
        v                          [Model 2: Misclassification-
[Model 1: Spread / Fisher-KPP        likelihood classifier]
 + stratified-diffusion MC]                   |
        |                                     v
        v                          [Model 3: Priority ranking /
  spatial risk prior  ------------->  resource allocation]
        |                                     |
        v                                     v
[Model 5: Eradication confidence   [Model 4: Update cadence /
 (CoA + Solow gap test)]            online refresh policy]
```

We deliberately do **not** try to force a single unified model to answer all five sub-questions — the spread process is a mechanistic ecological question, while misclassification and triage are statistical/operational questions about a report database. Section 2 lays out our assumptions; Section 3 develops each model with full mathematical notation; Section 4 presents illustrative numerical results; Section 5 is a sensitivity analysis; Section 6 discusses strengths/weaknesses; Section 7 concludes.

## 2. Assumptions and Justifications

| # | Assumption | Justification |
|---|---|---|
| A1 | Natural (non-human-assisted) queen dispersal is well-approximated, at the population level, by an isotropic 2-D random walk with a per-generation characteristic displacement equal to the 30 km range stated in the problem. | This is the only concrete dispersal-scale number given in the problem text; a random walk is the maximum-entropy (least additional-assumption) model for undirected dispersal absent trajectory data. |
| A2 | Locally, colonized area grows logistically (the species is univoltine — one generation per year, as stated), so that the combination of local growth and diffusion produces a traveling front satisfying the Fisher–KPP equation. | Fisher–KPP is the canonical, empirically validated model for "local growth + diffusion → constant-speed invasion front," used extensively in invasion ecology; it matches the biology described in the problem (queens overwinter, found new colonies each spring). |
| A3 | In addition to continuous diffusion, rare long-distance "jump" dispersal events occur, driven by inadvertent human transport (vehicles, cargo), and are modeled as a Poisson process with exponentially distributed jump distances. | This is the standard "stratified diffusion" mechanism from invasion ecology (Shigesada & Kawasaki) used whenever a species shows a two-scale spread pattern (steady local spread + occasional distant satellite colonies) — directly relevant since *V. mandarinia* itself cannot fly hundreds of kilometers but human transport can move it. |
| A4 | The four Lab Status categories (Positive ID, Negative ID, Unprocessed, Unverified) are collapsed to a binary label (Positive vs. Negative) for training; Unprocessed records are treated as unlabeled prediction targets, and Unverified records are treated as missing-information cases requiring separate handling — never silently relabeled as Negative. | Preserves the state's own operational semantics and avoids label leakage/bias from mistakenly treating "not yet checked" or "insufficient information" as confirmed negatives. |
| A5 | The free-text Notes field and structured fields (date, location, and any size/color descriptors) carry information statistically associated with the true label. | This is the necessary precondition for the problem's own request ("use the data set... to predict the likelihood of a mistaken classification") to be meaningful at all; it is also consistent with citizen-science verification literature, where description quality correlates with correct identification. |
| A6 | Marginal investigation cost is approximately equal across reports (a simplifying baseline; travel-distance-weighted costs are a natural extension). | Lets the resource-allocation problem reduce to a simple, transparent, interpretable greedy ranking rather than requiring integer programming machinery not justified by data we don't have. |
| A7 | Detection probability per unit surveillance effort in a given season, conditional on the population being present, is roughly stable within a season (though it may differ across seasons as effort/awareness changes). | Standard assumption in disease/pest-freedom surveillance theory (Martin et al., 2007), needed to make the confidence-of-absence calculation tractable and interpretable. |
| A8 | Confirmed detections, while the population is established, arrive approximately as a homogeneous Poisson process in time with rate λ. | Needed for the Solow (1993) sighting-gap cross-check; a Poisson process is the standard null model for "chance arrival of independent detection events" absent evidence of strong temporal clustering. |

## 3. Model Development

### 3.1 Notation

| Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| L | characteristic per-generation queen dispersal distance (km); L = 30 (given) |
| T | generation time (yr); T = 1 (univoltine) |
| D | diffusion coefficient (km²/yr), D = L²/(4T) |
| r | intrinsic local population growth rate (1/yr) |
| c\* | Fisher–KPP asymptotic invasion-front speed (km/yr), c\* = 2√(rD) |
| λ_jump | rate of long-distance human-mediated jump events (events/yr) |
| μ_jump | mean jump distance (km), jump distance ~ Exponential(μ_jump) |
| R(t) | maximum colonized radius from origin at year t (km) |
| x_i | feature vector for report i (structured + text-derived) |
| y_i ∈ {0,1} | true label for report i (1 = confirmed *V. mandarinia*) |
| p_i = P(y_i=1 \| x_i) | model-estimated probability report i is a true positive |
| p | per-season detection probability conditional on presence |
| CoA_n | confidence of absence after n consecutive negative seasons |
| λ | rate of confirmed detections while population is present (Solow test) |

### 3.2 Model 1 — Spatial Spread Prediction

**Diffusion coefficient from the given biology.** For a 2-D isotropic random walk, mean-square displacement after time T is ⟨R²⟩ = 4DT. Setting ⟨R²⟩ = L² with the given L = 30 km, T = 1 yr:

  D = L² / (4T) = 30² / 4 = **225 km²/yr**.

This is the one number in the model directly and rigorously anchored to the problem statement (no illustrative assumption involved).

**Mechanistic front speed.** Assuming local logistic growth with rate r superimposed on this diffusion, the population density n(x,t) is governed by the Fisher–KPP equation

  ∂n/∂t = D∇²n + r·n·(1 − n/K),

whose fronts converge to the asymptotic minimum wave speed

  c\* = 2√(rD).

Because r is not given in the problem, we report c\* across an illustrative range r ∈ {0.5, 1.0, 2.0} yr⁻¹, giving c\* ∈ {21.2, 30.0, 42.4} km/yr and hence 10-year colonized radii of {212, 300, 424} km under pure diffusion (no jumps). These are explicitly **not** predictions of the real invasion — they show how the model's output scales with a currently uncalibrated parameter, and give the order of magnitude one would expect once r is estimated from real confirmed-detection data.

**Stratified diffusion (adding human-mediated jumps).** Following the standard two-scale invasion model, at each year t we (i) grow every existing colony's radius by c\*·(t − birth year), and (ii) draw a Poisson(λ_jump) number of new "jump" colonies, each founded at a random point at distance ~ Exponential(μ_jump) and uniformly random direction from a randomly chosen existing colonized point. The colonized region at year t is the union of all growing disks; we track its maximum radius from the origin, R(t). We run this as a Monte Carlo simulation (N = 2000 replicates) to obtain the full predictive distribution of R(t), not just a point estimate — this is what lets us give a genuine, quantitative answer to "with what precision can spread be predicted."

### 3.3 Model 2 — Misclassification-Likelihood Classifier

We model P(y_i = 1 | x_i) with L2-regularized logistic regression:

  logit(p_i) = β₀ + β·x_i,   p_i = 1/(1 + e^{−(β₀+β·x_i)})

fit by maximizing the class-weighted log-likelihood (weights inversely proportional to class frequency, to counteract the extreme imbalance implied by the problem statement — "most reported sightings mistake other hornets" — without which the model would trivially predict "always negative"). Feature vector x_i combines:

- **Temporal**: month of sighting, encoded cyclically as (sin(2π·month/12), cos(2π·month/12)) to respect seasonality (the problem states new queens disperse in spring and leave the nest in fall — flight-season timing is diagnostic).
- **Spatial**: log(1 + distance to nearest previously confirmed detection), reflecting that true detections should spatially cluster near an actual population.
- **Morphological (from Notes / images)**: reported body size in mm; a "diagnostic keyword score" standing in for a bag-of-words/TF-IDF feature derived from the free-text Notes field (real implementation: term weights fit on the training corpus for words such as "orange," "giant," "two inches," "stripes," or trained via a lexicon of known *V. mandarinia* diagnostic traits from the Penn State Extension reference material listed as a problem attachment).

**Model selection rationale** (see Section thinking-log for the full comparison): we chose logistic regression over naive Bayes (too restrictive an independence assumption once we mix text and structured features), and over random forests / deep NLP (interpretability required for a government memo; expected extremely small positive-class sample size makes complex models prone to overfitting; logistic regression supports cheap incremental/online updates via stochastic gradient descent, directly relevant to sub-question 4).

**Calibration.** Because probabilities feed directly into resource-ranking (not just a binary decision), we assess calibration via reliability curves (predicted vs. observed frequency in quantile bins) and the Brier score, not just discrimination metrics like AUC.

### 3.4 Model 3 — Prioritization / Resource Allocation

Given a budget of B investigator-hours and (Assumption A6) approximately equal cost per investigation, the expected-value-maximizing policy under budget B is to investigate the top-⌊B/c⌋ reports ranked by p̂_i, where c is per-report cost. This is the greedy solution to the (relaxed, fractional) knapsack problem

  max Σ p̂_i·z_i   s.t.  Σ c·z_i ≤ B,  z_i ∈ [0,1],

whose optimal solution is exactly "sort by p̂_i/c and take as much as the budget allows" — under equal costs this is simply "rank by p̂_i and take the top K." If costs vary materially (e.g., travel distance), the natural extension is to rank by p̂_i / cost_i (still solvable by the same greedy argument for the fractional relaxation, or by dynamic programming for the exact 0/1 version) — we note this explicitly as a direct extension rather than re-deriving a new model.

### 3.5 Model 4 — Update Cadence

We treat the choice of refresh interval Δt as a constraint-satisfaction problem: choose the smallest Δt such that the **expected number of unscored true positives accumulating between refreshes stays below a tolerance** (we use tolerance = 1, i.e., "on average, no more than one true hornet report should sit un-prioritized under a stale model"):

  E[true positives per Δt] = (report arrival rate) × (assumed prevalence) × Δt ≤ 1
  ⟹ Δt ≤ 1 / (arrival rate × prevalence).

Because arrival rate is strongly seasonal (active flight season roughly April–November vs. dormant December–March, per the biology described in the problem), this bound is evaluated separately per season, yielding a season-dependent cadence recommendation rather than one fixed number.

### 3.6 Model 5 — Evidence of Eradication

**Confidence of absence (closed form).** Let p be the probability of detecting the population in a given surveillance season, conditional on it still being present. After n consecutive negative seasons, the probability that this run of silence occurred *even though* the population is still present is (1−p)ⁿ, so the confidence that the population is truly absent is

  CoA_n = 1 − (1 − p)ⁿ.

Given a target confidence level (e.g., 95%), the required number of consecutive negative seasons is n = ⌈ln(1 − target)/ln(1 − p)⌉ — directly computable, and explicitly dependent on the surveillance effort embedded in p (weak surveillance requires far more silent seasons before eradication can be credibly claimed).

**Solow (1993) sighting-gap cross-check.** If the population were still present, confirmed detections would arrive as a Poisson process with rate λ estimated from the historical inter-detection gaps while the population was known to be established. Given an observed silent gap of length G since the last confirmed detection, the probability of such a gap occurring by chance while still present is P(no detection in G | present) = e^{−λG}; a small value (e.g. < 0.05) is evidence against continued presence.

**Joint stopping rule.** We recommend declaring "no evidence of an established population" only when **both** criteria are satisfied — CoA_n ≥ 0.95 under the currently estimated p, **and** the Solow gap test rejects continued presence at α = 0.05 — because either criterion alone is fragile to its single underlying assumption (p or λ) being badly mis-estimated; requiring both guards against that fragility.

**Answer to "can spread be predicted, with what precision?"** The **median** trajectory tracks the deterministic diffusion prediction almost exactly (jump events are rare enough that the typical simulated path has none), so short-to-medium horizon *typical-case* prediction is fairly precise. But the **upper tail widens substantially** — by year 10 the 90% interval spans 300–465 km, a 55% relative spread driven almost entirely by whether a rare long-distance human-mediated jump occurred. Precision is therefore fundamentally **asymmetric**: a lower bound (diffusion-only) is fairly trustworthy, while an upper bound requires explicit uncertainty quantification over rare jump events and should never be reported as a single number.

### 4.2 Misclassification-likelihood classifier

On the illustrative synthetic dataset (N=4440, assumed positive prevalence 3%, deliberately overlapping class distributions to avoid an unrealistically "easy" separation):

- ROC-AUC = 0.982, PR-AUC (average precision) = 0.857 (vs. 0.030 baseline), Brier score = 0.0363.
- Fitted coefficient signs match the injected structure: month (seasonal), −log(distance to known detection), reported size, and keyword score all point in the expected direction.
- At an operating threshold tuned for 90% recall (chosen because missing a true hornet is operationally far costlier than a false alarm): precision = 0.522, false-alarm rate = 2.6%.

| λ_jump | mean jump (km) | Median R10 (km) | 90% CI |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0.20 | 40 | 300.0 | [300.0, 348.4] |
| 0.20 | 100 | 300.0 | [300.0, 555.7] |
| 0.40 | 60 (base) | 300.0 | [300.0, 467.2] |
| 0.40 | 100 | 350.3 | [300.0, 653.0] |
| 0.80 | 100 | 428.9 | [300.0, 736.8] |

**Finding**: the median stays within a narrow band (300–429 km, ≤ 1.4× variation) across the entire swept range, while the upper tail varies far more (up to 737 km). This confirms the qualitative conclusion from Section 4.1 — *typical-case prediction is robust to these uncalibrated parameters; worst-case prediction is not* — is not an artifact of one particular parameter choice.

### 5.2 Classifier: sensitivity to assumed prevalence

| Assumed prevalence | AUC | Precision @ recall ≥ 0.90 |
|---|---|---|
| 1% | 0.994 | 0.333 |
| 3% (base) | 0.987 | 0.537 |
| 6% | 0.986 | 0.649 |
| 10% | 0.982 | 0.750 |

**Finding**: AUC (a ranking-quality metric) is essentially prevalence-invariant, but precision at a fixed recall degrades sharply as true positives become rarer. This means the model's **ranking/triage** value is robust even without knowing the true prevalence in advance, but the **absolute false-alarm burden** placed on investigators is highly sensitive to it — reinforcing why we recommend reporting calibrated probabilities and a ranked list, rather than committing to a single fixed decision threshold, in the operational deployment.

## 6. Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths**
- The spread model directly and rigorously incorporates the one piece of hard biological data given in the problem (30 km dispersal range) rather than treating it as decorative context.
- The classifier is interpretable end-to-end, which matters for a government agency that must justify prioritization decisions.
- Every operational recommendation (update cadence, eradication threshold) is derived from an explicit, auditable quantitative criterion rather than an arbitrary round number.
- The sensitivity analysis explicitly separates "what is robust" (median spread trajectory, ranking AUC) from "what is fragile" (tail spread uncertainty, absolute precision), which is exactly the honest, decision-relevant information a regulator needs.

**Weaknesses**
- **No access to the real dataset**: every non-given numeric parameter is illustrative. The framework must be recalibrated on real confirmed-detection data before any output is operationally trustworthy.
- **No use of the image data**: the problem explicitly allows using submitted images; a complete solution would add an image-classification channel (e.g., a lightweight CNN or transfer-learning model) as a third feature source alongside text and structured fields. This was not implemented here due to the missing/oversized image archive.
- **Isotropic, homogeneous spatial assumptions**: real spread and reporting intensity are shaped by road networks, human population density, and land cover, none of which are modeled; the jump kernel's isotropic exponential form is a simplification.
- **Equal-cost assumption in the triage model**: a full solution should weight investigation cost by travel distance/logistics, turning the problem into a genuine (weighted) knapsack rather than a plain ranking.
- **Synthetic classifier data is illustrative of pipeline behavior only**, not an estimate of real-world achievable AUC/precision — a reader must not treat the reported 0.982 AUC as a claim about the actual system.

## 7. Conclusion

We built a two-track modeling framework for the Asian giant hornet problem: (1) a mechanistic reaction–diffusion / stratified-jump-diffusion model for spatial spread, directly anchored to the given 30 km dispersal biology, that yields both a point prediction and a genuinely quantified, asymmetric precision envelope; and (2) an interpretable, calibrated logistic-regression classifier for public-report triage that directly powers a resource-ranking policy, a principled update-cadence rule, and — combined with a closed-form confidence-of-absence calculation and a Solow sighting-gap cross-check — a rigorous, surveillance-intensity-dependent criterion for declaring eradication. In the absence of the official dataset, all illustrative numerical results were built to validate that this pipeline is internally consistent and produces sensible, interpretable, appropriately-uncertain output; the methodology is fully specified and ready to be recalibrated the moment real confirmed-detection and report data become available.

## Memorandum

**To:** Washington State Department of Agriculture
**From:** Modeling Team (illustrative/training exercise — see disclaimer)
**Re:** Predicting hornet spread and prioritizing sighting-report investigation

This memo summarizes actionable recommendations from our modeling study of the Asian giant hornet reporting and spread problem.

**1. Spread prediction.** Using the known 30 km/year queen dispersal range, we estimate a diffusion coefficient of 225 km²/yr. Combined with population growth assumptions (to be calibrated from your confirmed-detection records), our model gives a "typical case" colonized-radius trajectory that grows roughly linearly with time and is fairly precise in the near term. However, the *worst-case* spread envelope is substantially wider and driven almost entirely by rare, human-assisted long-distance transport events (e.g., via vehicles or cargo) rather than by the hornets' own flight range. **Recommendation**: budget surveillance resources for the typical-case radius, but maintain a rapid-response reserve capable of investigating sudden detections well outside the expected front — these are the signature of jump dispersal, not model failure.

**2. Report triage.** Because the vast majority of reports are misidentifications, we recommend scoring every incoming report with a calibrated probability model combining the report's date, distance to previously confirmed detections, described size, and diagnostic keywords/phrases in the free-text description. In our illustrative validation exercise, investigating only the highest-scored 5–10% of reports captured nearly all simulated true positives — at 10–17× the efficiency of unranked investigation. **Recommendation**: adopt a scored-ranking workflow rather than first-in-first-out or purely geographic triage.

**3. Update frequency.** Because report volume and hornet activity are strongly seasonal, we recommend refreshing the model **weekly during the active flight season (April–November)** and **monthly during the dormant season (December–March)**. This cadence is derived from keeping the expected backlog of unscored true-positive reports below one, not chosen arbitrarily.

**4. Declaring eradication.** We recommend against any fixed "N seasons with no reports" rule in isolation. Instead, the number of required silent seasons should be calculated from your actual surveillance detection probability (weaker surveillance requires more silent seasons — our illustrative table shows anywhere from 3 to 14 seasons depending on assumed detection strength) and cross-checked against a statistical test on the gap since the last confirmed detection. Both criteria should agree before eradication is declared.

We emphasize that the specific numbers in the accompanying paper are illustrative placeholders pending calibration on your real confirmed-detection dataset; the decision framework itself is ready for that calibration.
